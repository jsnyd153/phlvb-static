Understanding Levels

Making sure you are the proper level for an event is important for ensuring everyone stays safe and has a good time, but they can be confusing if you’re new to volleyball.

Why levels matter

Making sure you are the proper level for an event is important for ensuring everyone stays safe and has a good time, but they can be confusing if you’re new to volleyball.

From a fun perspective, it is important for players to have the level of play they expect. Everyone can have fun playign volleyball regardless of their level, but it can get dicey when the gap in skills levels get to far apart. Loosing over and over isn’t fun, and on the flipside, not getting to have long rallies isn't fun. Some events may have a wider range or all skill levels and tend to be more of a learning environment, but for many events having a relatively even skill level makes it more fun for everyone involved..

From a safety perspective, volleyball can be a fast paced game and having high level players spiking the ball at lower level players, or having players running into each other or contacting each other under the net can be unsafe.

Beginner → B → BB → A → AA → Open

This is the general ranking system used in the region, for tournaments and pickup.

While all versions of volleyball use the same core skills, there are slightly different descriptions for indoor(sixes) and doubles (doubles) since they prioritize different things — i.e. doubles (outdoors) prioritizes being a well rounded player and taking smart shots compared to sixes (indoor) prioritizes being more specialized and hitting with power.

Beginner Level (Recreational)

"Beginner" players are playing recreational level volleyball. They're still leanring how to play and the focus is mostly getting the ball back over the net rather than gettign 3 touches and bump/set/spike.

"Beginner" players are playing recreational level volleyball. Most beginner player will have a very hard time playing doubles as they have not developed the skills necessary to keep rallies going, but may be more comfortable with quads or sixes outdoors.

B Level

B level players are still in the early learning stages of the game but knows the skills. They understand the basic concepts of the game and try to get 3 touches on the ball each play. They know how to bump, set (a little), and spike, but have a lot of practice yet to go to be able to do these skills consistently. A "B" player makes mistakes often.

B players are not beginners, but are still learning the game. They should have a basic understanding of the how to play doubles but are still inconsistent in their core skills. They can set their partner, but many of their sets may be technically doubles contacts, or may be limited to bump setting. Teams should be able to get 3 contacts on the ball, but many plays result in either rolling or bumping the ball.

B level is the lowest level of competition, but it is still a level of competition at most tournaments. B is not the same as Beginner/Recreational level.

BB Level

BB players knows where to be on the court at all times when plays are developing. They know the footwork of and how to approach hitting effectively from an assigned position. A "BB" player knows how to run a 5-1 (or 6-2), and play a position in these systems.

Some events might differentiate between Upper and Lower BB

Upper BB is 80% consistent at every skill point (serving, passing, setting, and hitting.) You should be able to effectively play your position, not just know how. Players are hitting the ball hard at this level so front row players should be able to block at the net.

BB players are can execute all skills well (serving, receiving, setting, hitting) but vary in their consistency. They should be able to serve receive consistently and should have a strong understanding of defensive positioning and blocking calls. Hand sets should be clean (and you should have the ability to recognize double contacts.)

The core difference between Upper BB and Lower BB is the level of consistency and athleticism. Lower BB may have an understanding of how to play doubles, but are only executing on the core skills ~50-60% of the time and may be physically more limited. At the Upper BB you should be doing most skills well 70-80% of the time and have the athleticism to effectively cover the court and execute a large range of offensive tactics.

Upper BB players would expect to finish towards the top of BB tournaments.

A Level

A level players the skills needed to play at a high level. They have in depth knowledge of offensive and defensive game play, and use advanced strategies to be successful - they can do more than just hit a ball hard. They know how to play their specific possition verty well.

“A” level players are typically in excellent physical condition and are able to compete at a high level. These players are former club and/or college athletes, and are extremely competitive.

A level players are above average competitive players. They have in depth knowledge of offensive and defensive game play, and can consistently execute on all the core skills. When attacking they should be able to utilize a full range of offensive options.

At this level doubles players may become more specialized as blockers/defenders.

AA Level

"AA" players are superb athletes who execute their skills with the highest of power and efficiency. This is the last stop before becoming a "Pro" or "Open" player.

Open

Open refers to open level competition which is the highest level of competition offered at large tournaments. Players in this category are professional/semi-pro level players.
